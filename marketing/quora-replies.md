### 2. EFL 课堂词汇游戏

**问题：** [What are some great word games to play in class for learners of English as a foreign language?](https://www.quora.com/What-are-some-great-word-games-to-play-in-class-for-learners-of-English-as-a-foreign-language)

**建议正文（发布前可微调）：**

```
Charades is underrated for EFL because it forces meaning without translation drills.

Low-prep version I use:
- Split class into two teams
- Pull 8–10 words from the current unit (verbs and concrete nouns work best)
- One student acts at the front for 45–60 seconds; no talking, no props
- Class guesses; optional mini whiteboards for quieter students

Why it works: movement + guessing beats rote lists for retention. Shy students can start as guessers.

I've been using a free browser tool with a built-in timer and category filters: https://how-to-play-charades.com/en/blog/charades-for-classroom — it includes a full 15-minute lesson script if you want something structured on day one.

Pair it with Pictionary for variety; charades is better when you want zero materials and more energy.
```

### 3. 朋友与家人文字游戏

**问题：** [What are some good word games to play with friends and family?](https://www.quora.com/What-are-some-good-word-games-to-play-with-friends-and-family)

**建议正文（发布前可微调）：**

```
For mixed ages at home, my go-to stack is:

1. Charades — highest energy, zero setup. Your phone is the timer and word deck.
2. Codenames or Taboo — better for teens+ who like word association
3. Telephone — young kids love it

Charades scales well because grandparents guess while kids act. We use https://how-to-play-charades.com for themed lists (kids, holidays) and 60-second rounds — one person holds the phone, no app install.

Tip: agree on house rules first (especially "no props" vs costume charades at parties). Their rules page covers props, gestures, and scoring if anyone argues mid-game.
```
